Animals

Panthera Uncia

Snow Leopard

The Snow Leopard is one of the largest animals to live in the Himalayas. The Snow Leopard is a carnivorous animal with an average weight of 72 pounds. Don't worry though, Snow Leopards are usually not hostile towards humans compared to other big cats, a small weapon or reppelant would be reccomended.

Pseudois Nayaur

Himalayan Blue Sheep

The Himalayan Blue Sheep is native to the high Himalayas of India, Nepal, Bhutan, Tibet, and Pakistan. The blue sheep is an herbavore and is usually not a threat to travelers. The blue sheep is one of the largest food sources of the Snow Leopard.

Plants

Cedrus deodara

Himalayan Screw Pine

The Himalayan screw pine is native to the Himalayan region. It grows on shady slopes between 300 and 1500 meters. The screw pine drops fruits up to 25 centimeters long and is NOT poisonous.

Rosa moschata

Musk Rose

The Musk Rose is a shrub with 5 centimeter white flowers. It is reported that its wild origins lie in the western himalayas. It has been contended that no truly wild examples of the musk rose have been found, though it is recorded in cultivation as least as far back as the 16th century.

Weather & Climate

  • The Himalayas are very diverse in climate.
  • Humid, subtropical foothills.
  • Dry desert conditions on the Tibetan side of the range.
  • The Himalayas receive about 300 mm of precipitation per year. Most of this is in the form of snowfall.
  • In the southern foothills, summer temperatures can reach a tropical 86 degrees Fahrenheit.
  • Temperatures in the high peaks of the mountain range can reach a staggering -50 degrees Fahrenheit or lower in winter.
  • If you plan on climbing the upper mountains in the range, you definitely would go in the summer or spring months.
  • You should avoid visiting the northern extreme elevation section of the Himalayas to avoid sub-zero temperatures, avalanches, and risk of cold and altitude induced illnesses such as hypothermia or hypoxia.
  • Vacationers need to pack extremely warm and insulating coats and pants for mountain climbing, and light jackets and pants for the southern subtropical section of the Himalayas.

Geography

  • The Himalayan Mountain Range is over 2,400 kilometers in length.
  • Obviously, the highest point in the mountain range is Mount Everest.
  • The mountain range has over 50 other mountains that exceed 7,200 meters in height.
  • Parts of the range are experiencing an uplift of up to 1 centimeter per year!
  • The Himalayan range is made up of three parallel ranges often referred to as the Greater Himalayas, the Lesser Himalayas, and the Outer Himalayas.

Conclusion & Places to Visit

Conclusion

The Himalayas are a wonderful travel destination for those willing to take the challenge. The sub-zero temperatures in winter and extremely remote tourist attractions may deter some tourists.
